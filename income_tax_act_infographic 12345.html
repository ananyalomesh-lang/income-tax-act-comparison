Two Statutes, One Timeline

The Income-tax Act, 1961 governed Indian direct taxation for over six decades. The Income-tax Act, 2025 does not replace its tax policy, it rebuilds the same policy in a cleaner shell.

The Core Comparison

Read side by side: the inherited structure of 1961 against the consolidated form of 2025.

Enacted 1961 · in force till 31.03.2026

Income-tax Act, 1961

  • 819+ sections incl. alphabetical suffixes (80C, 194B, etc.)
  • Twin concept: Previous Year + Assessment Year
  • TDS spread across 60+ sections (192 – 206CCA)
  • Deductions scattered across Ch. VI-A, Sec. 10(14), 24, etc.
  • 4,000+ amendments across 65 Finance Acts, dense provisos
  • ITR-U (updated return) window: 24 months
Enacted 2025 · in force from 01.04.2026

Income-tax Act, 2025

  • 536 sequentially numbered sections, 16 schedules
  • Single concept: Tax Year (1 Apr – 31 Mar)
  • TDS consolidated into Sec. 392 (salary), 393 (other), 394 (TCS)
  • Deductions regrouped into unified chapters & tables
  • ~1,200 provisos / 900 explanations absorbed or removed (~40% shorter)
  • ITR-U window extended to 48 months

What Actually Changes

The changes are structural and terminological — not changes to the tax burden itself.

01Previous Year + AY → Tax Year

The dual-year system that confused first-time filers is gone. One 12-month period is now used both for earning and assessing income.

PY + AY→Tax Year

02TDS/TCS consolidation

Instead of hunting across 60+ sections, all TDS rates, thresholds and payment types sit in structured tables under three sections.

Sec. 192–206CCA→Sec. 392–394

03Deductions regrouped

80C, 80D, 80G and similar deductions are retained in substance but renumbered and grouped by head of income for easier navigation.

Ch. VI-A (80C…)→Sec. 122–154

04Unified compliance forms

Multiple audit and TDS return forms are streamlined into single numbered forms, reducing duplication in filing formats.

Form 3CA/3CB/16/24Q→Form 26 / 130 / 138

What Stays Exactly the Same

  • Income tax slabs & rates under old and new tax regimes
  • Sec. 87A rebate — zero tax up to ₹12 lakh (new regime)
  • Standard deduction of ₹75,000 for salaried taxpayers
  • Core charging principle: tax on total income of the year
  • Underlying deduction limits (80C, 80D ceilings, etc.)
  • Assessments and proceedings completed before 1 April 2026

Why It Matters

Implications differ by who is filing and when.

StakeholderImplication
Salaried individualsNo change to tax owed; Form 16 and section references change only from Tax Year 2026–27 onward.
Businesses / deductorsPayroll and TDS software must be remapped to Sec. 392–394; rates and thresholds are unchanged.
Professionals / auditorsMust learn new form numbering (Form 26 replacing 3CA/3CB/3CD) and the old-to-new section mapping.
Students & researchersNeed fluency in both frameworks during the transition years, since pre-2026 matters stay under the 1961 Act.
Policy viewDemonstrates that simplification of drafting can be pursued independently of changing tax policy.
IMP

Transitional rule to remember: Income earned up to 31 March 2026 (including FY 2025–26, filed in July 2026) is governed entirely by the Income-tax Act, 1961, as amended by the Finance Act, 2025. The Income-tax Act, 2025 applies only from Tax Year 2026–27 onward. Assessments and pending proceedings for earlier years continue undisturbed under transitional provisions.
